Introduction

Minestrone is a hearty, nutritious Italian soup filled with fresh vegetables, beans, and pasta or rice. It’s a celebration of seasonal produce and rustic Italian cooking that warms the soul and satisfies the appetite.

History

The origins of minestrone trace back to ancient Roman times, when meals were largely plant-based and made with whatever ingredients were readily available. The word "minestrone" comes from "minestra," meaning soup. Over time, it evolved into a regional favorite across Italy, with each area giving its own twist based on local ingredients.

Why We Love It

Minestrone is versatile, comforting, and packed with fiber and plant-based protein. It's budget-friendly, freezer-friendly, and endlessly customizable. You can make it year-round and tailor it to your taste and dietary needs!

Ingredients

  • 2 tablespoons olive oil
  • 1 onion, diced
  • 2 garlic cloves, minced
  • 2 carrots, chopped
  • 2 celery stalks, chopped
  • 1 zucchini, chopped
  • 1 cup green beans, chopped
  • 1 can (14 oz) diced tomatoes
  • 1 can (15 oz) cannellini beans, drained and rinsed
  • 4 cups vegetable broth
  • 1 teaspoon dried oregano
  • 1 teaspoon dried basil
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • 1/2 cup small pasta (like ditalini or elbow)
  • Fresh parsley or Parmesan for garnish (optional)

Instructions

  1. Heat olive oil in a large pot over medium heat. Sauté the onion and garlic for 2–3 minutes until softened.
  2. Add the carrots, celery, zucchini, and green beans. Cook for 5–7 minutes, stirring occasionally.
  3. Stir in the diced tomatoes, cannellini beans, vegetable broth, oregano, and basil. Season with salt and pepper.
  4. Bring to a boil, then reduce heat and simmer uncovered for 20–25 minutes.
  5. Add the pasta and cook for 8–10 minutes, or until pasta is tender.
  6. Serve hot, garnished with fresh parsley or grated Parmesan if desired.

Tips & Tricks

  • Use seasonal vegetables for the freshest flavor and texture.
  • Add a Parmesan rind while simmering for extra depth (remove before serving).
  • Use gluten-free pasta or rice to make it gluten-free.
  • Double the batch and freeze in portions—just leave out the pasta and add it fresh when reheating.
  • A splash of lemon juice or vinegar before serving brightens the flavors.

Serving Suggestions

Pair minestrone soup with a slice of warm crusty bread or focaccia and a drizzle of good olive oil. A side of Italian salad or a glass of red wine like Sangiovese rounds out this comforting meal beautifully.

Fun Fact

Minestrone has no fixed recipe—it varies greatly by region and season. In Liguria, it’s common to stir in a spoonful of pesto for added richness and aroma!
